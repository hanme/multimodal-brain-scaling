Summary Auditory stimulus blocks were presented to 6 subjects. 80% of the stimuli in each block were standards of 1000 Hz 
and 20% were deviants of either 1002 Hz, 1004 Hz, 1008 Hz, 1016 Hz or 1032 Hz, one deviant type in each block. The constant 
interstimulus interval was 1 sec and the order of the stimuli was randomized. The subject was instructed either to ignore the deviant 
stimuli (ignore condition) or to press a response key to them (discrimination condition). 
In the ignore condition, an ERP component called the mismatch negativity (MMN), with a peak latency of approximately 170 
msec, was elicited by those deviants exceeding the discrimination threshold (1016 Hz and 1032 Hz) and also those at the threshold 
(1008 Hz) tended to elicit a small MMN. In the discrimination condition, in addition to MMN, another negative component, N2b, 
was elicited by the detected deviants. This component had a somewhat longer latency than, and its midline distribution was posterior 
to, the MMN. 
The present results are in line with the hypothesis according to which the MMN component reflects the activation of cerebral 
mechanisms of passive discrimination, those which cause us to become aware of occasional changes in unattended stimulus sequences. 
In the discrimination condition, N2b and the slow parietal positivity were dominant features of the ERPs elicited by the detected 
suprathreshold deviants. The data obtained at the discrimination threshold specifically associate the parietal positivity with becoming 
aware of stimulus change since those deviants which were detected elicited this positivity whereas there was none to those (physically 
identical) deviants which remained undetected.

Event-related brain potentials (ERPs) in re- 
sponse to auditory stimuli have proved useful in 
studying brain mechanisms underlying human 
auditory stimulus discrimination (Donchin et al. 
1978; Ritter et al. 1979; Curry et al. 1983; 
Fitzgerald and Picton 1983). In the basic 'oddball' 
paradigm the subject is instructed to discriminate 
infrequent and unpredictable deviant stimuli pre- 
sented among frequent 'standard' stimuli. In com- 
parison to the latter, the detected deviant stimuli 
elicit a negative deflection (N200 or N2) with a 
peak latency of approximately 200 msec, followed 
by a positive P300 (P3) deflection (for a review, 
see Donchin et al. 1978). The latencies of both

ignore condition where only MMN should be seen.

ously shown that MMN (N200 in the ignore con- 
dition) is elicited by quite small suprathreshold

Method and Material 
Main experiment 
Six university students (22-31 years, 5 males) 
served as subjects. Experiments were carried out in 
an electrically and acoustically shielded room. 
Tones produced by 50 msec bursts of sine wave 
(rise-time 1 cycle), with an intensity of about 80 
dB (SPL), were presented in blocks of 500 stimuli 
to the subject's right ear at the constant repetition 
rate of 1 Hz. There were tones of two frequencies 
in each block: standard tones of 1000 Hz at the 
probability of 0.8 and deviant tones of either 1002 
Hz, 1004 Hz, 1008 Hz, 1016 Hz, or 1032 Hz at the 
complementary probability of 0.2. The order of 
the stimuli was randomized. 
The experiment consisted of ignore and dis- 
crimination conditions which were run on separate 
days. In the ignore condition, the subject was 
instructed to read comics and to ignore the audi- 
tory stimuli and in the discrimination condition to 
press the response key with the fight index finger 
always when he was sure that the stimulus was a 
deviant. There were two blocks for each deviant 
frequency in each condition. In the discrimination 
condition there were two additional blocks with 
1032 Hz deviants which the subject counted si- 
lently. The order of the blocks was randomized 
and the conditions were counterbalanced. 
The EEG was recorded with Ag-AgC1 elec- 
trodes at Fz, Cz and Pz, referred to the combined 
ear lobes (A1-A2). The subject was grounded at 
his left wrist. The vertical EOG was recorded 
between two electrodes above and below the sub- 
ject's right eye. The EEG and EOG were amplified 
(bandpass 0.1-100 Hz, -3 dB points) and stored 
on a magnetic tape together with trigger pulses for 
the computer analysis. The trigger pulse preceded the stimulus onset by 60 msec and started the 
analysis period of 522 msec. The computer auto- 
matically omitted the epochs with eye movements 
exceeding + 75 ~tV. 
The ERPs at each derivation were averaged 
separately for the deviants and standards, and in 
the discrimination condition separately for cor- 
rectly rejected standards, false-alarm standards, 
detected deviants and missed deviants. The EEG 
epochs following a deviant or a standard preceded 
by less than two standards were omitted from the 
averaging. Amplitude measurements were made in 
reference to the 60 msec pre-stimulus baseline. The 
statistical analysis of the data consisted of a two- 
way analysis of variance, followed by a Newman- 
Keuls post-hoc test to determine the significance 
of the differences between the single means. 
Two small supplementary experiments were 
conducted. The purpose of the first was to expand 
the critical data basis for determining the sensitiv- 
ity of the MMN to stimulus deviations. In the 
second, the reading task of the ignore condition 
was replaced by a visual computer game to ensure 
that the subject really ignored the auditory stimuli. 
First supplementary experiment 
Four subjects were employed, two of whom 
participated also in the main experiment. The de- 
viant stimuli were 1004 Hz and 1008 Hz, each run 
in 6 blocks in random order. Additionally 3 blocks 
with 1016 Hz deviants were given to the two new 
subjects at the beginning of the experiment. Only 
the ignore condition was used. All the other ex- 
perimental details were the same as in the main 
experiment. 
The differences between the ERPs to the stan- 
dards and deviants were tested within subjects, 
using a one-tailed t test for independent samples. 
The degrees of freedom were determined by the 
number of summations (N) for the standards and 
deviants, respectively. 
Second supplementary experiment 
Two subjects of the main experiment were used. 
The stimuli were bursts of square waves (30 msec, 
70 dB SPL, rise time 1 cycle). The probabilites of 
the deviants and standards were 0.1 and 0.9, re- 
spectively. Four deviant frequencies were used:

1003 Hz, 1008 Hz (1009 Hz for the other subject 
for his slightly higher discrimination threshold), 
1032 Hz and 1250 Hz. Two blocks with each 
deviant value were presented in random order. In 
the ignore condition the subject performed a de- 
manding visual detection task in which he searched 
for a designated letter in a string of 15 randomly 
occurring letters. The target letter was displayed

string presentation. The subject pressed one of the

mediately. In the discrimination condition, the subject was instructed to press one response key

whether a deviant or a standard was presented. 
When he was sure that a standard occurred he 
pressed neither key.

N200 deflections in the ignore and discrimination 
conditions differ in the latency, scalp distribution 
and, perhaps, in amplitude also. The ignore N200 
is earlier, more anteriorly distributed and smaller 
than the discrimination N200. The ignore N200 is, 
apparently, composed mainly of the MMN com- 
ponent (see Introduction). In the discrimination 
condition, the N200 deflection evidently reflects

Discussion 
Separability of MMN and N2b 
The present results corroborate the existence of 
two negative components, MMN and N2b, in 
response to deviant stimuli. When the subjects 
ignored the auditory stimuli only MMN was 
elicited. This MMN had a frontal amplitude maxi- 
mum. In the discrimination condition the deviants additionally elicited the N2b component, which 
was of longer latency than MMN and, apparently, 
more posteriorly distributed. 
Sensitivity of MMN to stimulus deviation 
In the main experiment, analysed over the sub- 
jects, a significant MMN was elicited by slightly 
suprathreshold deviant stimuli whereas no signifi- 
cant MMN was observed at the discrimination 
threshold, though a tendency in this direction can 
be seen (Figs. 1 and 2). In the First Supplementary 
Experiment with improved signal-to-noise ratio, a 
significant MMN was obtained to the 1008 Hz 
deviants in two subjects out of four. No significant 
MMN was, however, observed to these deviants in 
the Second Supplementary Experiment, which may 
be due to an elevated discrimination threshold 
caused by employing shorter stimuli, produced by 
square rather than sine waves. Hence it appears 
that MMN of small amplitude tend to be elicited 
at the threshold, but this issue should be further 
clarified in future studies. 
Our results clearly show that the generation 
mechanism of MMN is much more sensitive to 
frequency changes than that of N100; the 1016 Hz 
and 1032 Hz deviants elicited a large MMN 
whereas the N100s to these deviants did not differ 
significantly from those to the standards. 
Functional role of the MMN generator mechanism 
We suggest, as did N~i~t~nen (1979, 1985), that 
the MMN is generated by a cerebral process un- 
derlying passive stimulus discrimination, that 
makes us aware of occasional changes in un- 
attended stimulus sequences. Hence we propose 
that the generation of MMN is related to the 
orienting effect of a deviant stimulus: the MMN 
system automatically sends an 'interrupt' to the 
limited-capacity system whose ongoing activity 
may then be terminated, at least briefly (see Ohman 
1979). This explanation is in line with the subjects' 
introspective post-session reports that even when 
they were truly engaged in the reading task, every 
now and then they became aware of the occur- 
rence of a deviant stimulus. This might be re- 
flected in the small P3a kind of positivity follow- 
ing MMN elicited by the 1016 Hz and 1032 Hz 
deviants. The functional role suggested for the 

446 M. SAMS ET AL. 
MMN generator system obviously resembles the 
neuronal mismatch process postulated by Sokolov's 
(1963, 1975) orienting reflex theory (see N~t~nen 
and Gaillard 1983) though there are important 
differences (N~tt~men 1984). 
When the subject is performing a frequency 
discrimination task, deviant stimuli are, ap- 
parently, detected by operation of some other 
cerebral mechanism than that reflected by MMN. 
This conclusion is supported by the fact that MMN 
is no longer elicited when the interstimulus inter- 
val in the stimulus block approaches 10 sec (see 
Mantysalo and N~tt~men, in prep.) whereas the 
frequency discrimination accuracy deteriorates 
much more mildly as a function of ISI, remaining 
quite high even beyond 10 sec (Harris 1952). 
In terms of cognitive psychology, MMN might 
reflect a brain process which belongs to those 
underlying automatic, in contrast to controlled, 
information processing. Automatic processing is 
characterized as fast, fairly effortless, parallel 
processing which is not under direct subject con- 
trol (Schneider et al. 1984). Lack of effort and 
independence of direct subject control in occur- 
rence of the brain process reflected by MMN is 
suggested by its elicitation during reading as well 
as in even more involving primary visual tasks. 
Such results also suggest that the MMN generator 
process occurs in parallel with other on-going in- 
formation processing activity. A comparison of 
MMNs in the ignore and discrimination condi- 
tions would be useful here but this could not be 
made since, in the discrimination condition, MMN 
was overlapped by N2b (see also Fitzgerald and 
Picton 1983). However, there are some other data 
(Ford et al. 1976; N~it~men et al. 1978, 1980, 
1982, 1983; Vaughan et al. 1980) which provide a 
basis to infer that the generator process of MMN 
is independent of attention. 
Discrimination condition 
The deviants above the discrimination threshold 
elicited both MMN and N2b components, which 
confirmed the previous results (N~t~nen et al. 
1982, 1983; Sams et al. 1984). The observed shor- 
tening of the N2b latency as a function of the 
magnitude of the deviation is in accordance with 
the previous literature (Ritter et al. 1979; Fitzgerald and Picton 1983; for a review, see N~at~inen and 
Gaitlard 1983). 
The slow parietal positivity was elicited by both 
suprathreshold deviants. At the threshold (1008 
Hz), these data are of particular interest. Positivity 
was triggered by the detected deviants whereas 
there was none in response to the physically iden- 
tical deviations which remained undetected. On 
the contrary, the latter ERPs showed a frontally 
maximal slow negativity which was statistically 
significant in reference to the ERP elicited by the 
standards.
