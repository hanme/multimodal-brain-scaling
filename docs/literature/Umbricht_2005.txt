Abstract
Background : Mismatch negativity (MMN) is an auditory event-related potential that provides an index of automatic context-
dependent information processing and auditory sensory memory. Many studies have reported abnormalities in the generation ofMMN in schizophrenia. The objective of this study was to assess the magnitude of this deficit and associated factors.
Method : Studies of MMN in schizophrenia were identified and included in a meta-analysis to estimate the mean effect size.
Effects of duration of illness, gender ratio, age of patients, type of MMN (duration versus frequency MMN) and characteristicsof the test paradigms (deviant probability, magnitude of standard-deviant difference) on effect size were assessed.Results : Of 62 identified studies 32 met our inclusion criteria. The mean effect size was 0.99 (95% confidence intervals: 0.79,
1.29). Overall, no specific factor was significantly associated with MMN deficits, although MMN to stimuli differing in
duration appeared more impaired in schizophrenia than MMN to frequency deviants. In addition, effect sizes of frequencyMMN were significantly correlated with duration of illness.
Conclusions : MMN deficits are a robust feature in chronic schizophrenia and indicate abnormalities in automatic context-
dependent auditory information processing and auditory sensory memory in these patients. Reports of normal MMN in first-episode schizophrenia and the association of deficits in frequency MMN with illness duration suggest that MMN may index
ongoing neuropathological changes in the auditory cortex in schizophrenia.

MMNis an auditory event-related potential (ERP) that isgenerated when a stimulus violates the invariance orregularity of the recent auditory past. In the mostsimple paradigm this is the case when an infrequent
stimulus (deviant) that differs in any physical
characteristic such as frequency, duration, intensityor location is presented among repeatedly presentedstandard stimuli ( Na¨a¨ta¨nen, 1995 ). In addition,
violations of more abstract regularities (repetitionof a stimulus in series of alternating tones, a lowertone in series of ascending tones) not directlyrelated to the physical characteristics of the stimuli
generate MMN ( Alain et al., 1999; Na ¨a¨ta¨nen et al.,
1993; Paavilainen et al., 1998; Schro ˜ger et al., 1996;
Tervaniemi et al., 1994 ). Thus, MMN generation
depends on the context in which a specific stimulusis presented. MMN is only generated when amemory trace of the regularity or invariance hasbeen established against which any incomingstimulus in being compared. MMN is therefore
considered an index of the auditory sensory or
bechoic Qmemory and of context-dependent informa-
tion processing at the level of the primary andsecondary auditory cortices. MMN indexes the firststep in a chain of events leading to the consciousdetection of differences between auditory stimuliand variance in the auditory environment.
MMN is best observed in the difference wave
between the ERP responses to the standard and
deviant stimuli (see Fig. 1 ) and usually peaks between
100 and 240 ms after the presentation of the deviantstimulus. Its maximum is seen over fronto-centralelectrodes and it reverses its polarity over themastoids in a nose-referenced montage. Larger differ-ences–for instance in frequency–between standardsand deviants and lower probability of the deviant are
associated with greater MMN amplitude ( Na¨a¨ta¨nen,
1992 ). The latter effect is believed to reflect the
strengthening of the memory trace of the standardstimulus, which is one factor determining MMNgeneration.
The generation of MMN is considered to be a fully
automatic process and therefore independent of thesubject’s attention ( Na¨a¨ta¨nen, 1992; Na ¨a¨ta¨nen et al.,1993 ); However, some studies have reported that
under exceptional circumstances attention can indi-rectly modulate MMN ( Sussman et al., 1998; Trejo et
al., 1995 ). The main MMN generators reside in the
primary and secondary auditory cortices as indicatedby intracranial recordings in animals and humans(Cse´pe, 1995; Cse ´pe et al., 1995; Javitt et al., 1996;
Kraus et al., 1994; Kropotov et al., 1995 ), magneto-
encephalographic (MEG) recordings and source local-
ization in humans ( Alho, 1995; Hari et al., 1984 ).
Since the first report of abnormal MMN in
schizophrenia by Shelley et al. in 1991 there have
been at least 40 additional studies on MMN inschizophrenia. This review summarises all publishedandbin print Qreports on MMN in schizophrenia up
to the end of 2003. It evaluates MMN findings in
general and with regard to specific features of the
samples investigated and the stimulation paradigmsused.
1. Methods
1.1. Literature search
A literature search was conducted in MEDLINE
(years 1990–2003) using the following key words:schizophrenia, mismatch negativity, MMN. Also, thereference lists of published papers were searched inorder to manually locate other relevant studies. Theinitial search identified 62 articles.
1.2. Inclusion and exclusion criteria
For inclusion in the meta-analysis a study had to
meet following criteria: (1) MMN data were reportedeither as mean and standard deviation, or alternatively,ast-value of a t-test or as F-value representing the
group effect when comparing patients with schizo-phrenia and controls; (2) patients were diagnosed
according to established diagnostic systems (DSM-III,
DSM-IV, ICD 9, ICD 10); (3) MMN values weremeasured from a difference wave (ERP to the deviantstimulus minus ERP to the standard stimulus). [Theonly study that did not fulfil this requirement, but wasincluded in the meta-analysis was the study by Javitt
et al. (1993) . This exception was made because the
rate of stimulus presentation was 20/s in which caseD. Umbricht, S. Krljes / Schizophrenia Research 76 (2005) 1–23 2

1.4. Publication bias
To address potential publication bias we calculated
thebfail-safe NQ(N
fs)—the number of non-significant
studies necessary in order to overturn the significant
results of the published studies.
We also applied the btrim and fill Qmethod, which
provides an empirical approach to estimate the effectsize if supposedly unpublished studies with negativeresults had been published ( Duval and Tweedie,
2000 ). Basically, this method estimates iteratively
how many of the studies with the largest effect sizesshould be added to the sample as studies with equalbut opposite ES. Then the new btrueQoverall ES is
recalculated. Thus, the result is an estimate of the trueES if the studies with the strongest ES were mirroredby studies of equal, but opposite ES.

2.2. Effect sizes
The individual effect sizes and their 95% con-
fidence intervals (CI) for each study are plotted inFig. 2 . Further details are given in Table 2 . Overall,
the effect size of the difference in MMN amplitudebetween patients and controls was 0.99 (lower 95% CI
0.79; upper 95% CI 1.29; see Fig. 2 ) indicating that
the MMN deficits are a robust feature in patients withschizophrenia. According to Cohen’s benchmark anES of 0.99 is classified as a large effect size ( Cohen,
1988 ).
2.3. Publication bias
Inspection of the funnel plot suggested a possible
publication bias (see Fig. 3 ). We thus applied the
bTrim and Fill Qmethod ( Duval and Tweedie, 2000 ).
This iterative method provides an estimate of thepotentially unpublished studies (variables L0 and R0,seeDuval and Tweedie, 2000 for details). In the case
of our data L0 was zero, R0 was equal to 4 andremained at this level in further iterations. We thusadded the four studies with the largest ES, but with a
negative sign to the original data and recalculated themean ES. The resulting mean ES was 0.65 (95% CI:0.37, 0.93, see Fig. 3 ). Thus, even after correcting for
a potential publication bias the mean ES remainedsignificant and robust.
The fail-safe Nfor this meta-analysis was 142.
Thus, 142 studies with non-significant findings
are required for the effect size of 0.99 to become
non-significant.
According to Cohen’s overlap statistics (OL%)
(Cohen, 1988 ) the obtained effects size of 0.99 means
that there is a 44% of overlap between schizophrenicpatients and healthy controls. This means that 56% ofschizophrenic patients have lower MMN amplitudesthan any healthy control.

3. Discussion
The results of our meta-analysis indicate that
MMN deficits are robust in patients with chronic
schizophrenia. The estimate of the overall effect size
was 0.99, which is considered large by acceptedstandards. Even after applying the so-called btrim and
fillQmethod to correct for potentially unpublished data
the ES remained robust. Given the evidence thatMMN deficits have not been observed in other majorpsychiatric disorders such as major depression andbipolar disorder ( Catts et al., 1995; Umbricht et al.,
2003b ), the results of this meta-analysis indicate a
strong association between chronic schizophrenia anddeficient MMN generation. Thus, the results of thismeta-analysis strongly suggest that the functioning ofthe auditory sensory memory and context-dependentinformation processing at the level of the primary andsecondary auditory cortices are significantly impairedin chronic schizophrenia. This supports the view that
so-called cognitive deficits in schizophrenia not only
include deficient performance in classical neuropsy-chological tasks, but also extend to early steps incortical information processing. Indeed, recent studieshave shown that MMN deficits are particularlypronounced in patients with severe cognitive deficits,particularly deficits in episodic memory ( Baldeweg
et al., 2004; Umbricht et al., 2004; Krljes et al.,
submitted for publication ). Thus, MMN deficits may
index abnormalities at the cellular level that contributesimilarly to both deficits in bclassical Qepisodic
memory as well as abnormal auditory sensorymemory.
The exact reasons for MMN deficits in schizo-
phrenia are not well understood. Since ERPs resultfrom EPSPs and IPSPs mostly in the dentritic arbour
of simultaneously active neuronal ensembles, an
obvious reason could lie in dentritic abnormalities,including loss of spines, previously described inschizophrenia. Indeed, emerging evidence supportsthis assumption ( Salisbury et al., 2004 ). In addition,
there is considerable evidence from animal and humanstudies implicating deficient NMDA receptor func-tioning in MMN deficits. Javitt et al. (1996) found adose dependent reduction of intracortically recorded
MMN by competitive and non-competitive NMDAreceptor antagonists in subhuman primates. Theseinitial findings have been extended to humans inwhom the NMDA receptor antagonist ketamine hasbeen found to reduce MMN similarly ( Kreitschmann-
Andermahr et al., 2001; Umbricht et al., 2000, 2004 ).
In addition, other compounds with NMDA antagonist
properties such as alcohol and nitric oxide induce an
MMN reduction ( Jaaskelainen et al., 1995; Pang and
Fowler, 1999 ). In contrast, the administration in
healthy volunteers of agonists and antagonists atvarious receptors did not reduce MMN generation:the tested drugs included psycilocybin, a psychotomi-metic acting as agonist at the 5-HT2a receptor(Umbricht et al., 2003c ), the alpha2-antagonist atipa-
mezole ( Mervaala et al., 1993 ), the noradrenergic
apha2-agonist clonidine ( Duncan and Kaye, 1987 ),
the D2 antagonist haloperidol ( Kahkonen et al., 2002 ),
the opioid antagonist naltrexone ( Jaaskelainen et al.,
1998 ) and caffeine ( Rosburg et al., 2004b ). In
addition, acute tryptophan depletion (ATD), whichdecreases 5-HT synthesis in the brain, did not reduceMMN amplitudes ( Ahveninen et al., 2002 ) nor did
growth hormone responses to clonidine and apomor-
pine challenges–as a proxy for noradrenergic anddopaminergic stimulation–correlate with MMNchanges ( Hansenne et al., 2003 ). The administration
of scopolamine, a cholinergic antagonist, did reducefrequency, but not duration MMN rendering this effectquestionable ( Pekkonen et al., 2001 ). A possible
involvement of the GABA receptor in MMN gen-
eration is suggested by the reduction of MMN
recorded in the morning following evening admin-istration of benzodiazepine triazolam ( Nakagome et
al., 1998 ). However, the benzodiazepine antagonist
flumazenil had no effect on MMN ( Smolnik et al.,
1998 ) nor did MMN differ between patients on or off
benzodiazepines ( Murakami et al., 2002 ) or on high
versus low doses of benzodiazepines ( Kasai et al.,
2002b ). In conclusion, the available evidence strongly
suggests a pivotal role of the NMDA receptor inMMN generation. Interestingly, dendritic spines are aprimary location of NMDA receptors ( Nimchinsky et
al., 2002 ). Thus, the described loss of spines in
schizophrenia could easily result in a functionallydeficient signal transmission through NMDA recep-tors and associated MMN deficits. Furthermore, anD. Umbricht, S. Krljes / Schizophrenia Research 76 (2005) 1–23 15

to MMN deficits–would also be expected to nega-tively impact many cognitive functions, particularlyepisodic memory, given the role of NMDA receptorsin learning and memory.
In our evaluation of moderator variables none was
associated with a significant effect. However, given theheterogeneity among the studies with regard to all the
parameters affecting MMN it remains questionable if a
meta-analysis can reveal more than extremely strongeffects. Nevertheless, some trends emerged which areworth discussing. The ES for MMN to durationdeviants was about 40% larger than the ES forfrequency MMN. For those studies investigating bothduration and frequency MMN this difference was even114%. Furthermore, in a multiple regression only
MMN type (frequency versus duration MMN) was
significantly associated with MMN ES. Thus, theseresults would support the hypothesis–originally putforward by Michie and colleagues ( Michie, 2001 )–that
duration MMN is more deficient in schizophrenia thanfrequency MMN. This research group consistentlydemonstrated large deficits in duration MMN in theircohorts of schizophrenic patients and an association of
MMN reduction and deficient performance on an
interval discrimination task in chronic schizophrenia(Michie, 2001; Todd et al., 2003 ). Similar findings in
schizophrenia patients were reported previously byother studies ( Rammsayer, 1990 ) examining the
behavioral indices of temporal processing dysfunctionin schizophrenia. While the available evidence issuggestive of a more pronounced deficit in duration
MMN in schizophrenia the proof will require studies in
which frequency and duration differences have beenpsychophysically matched in the normal control group.
Finally, our analysis suggests that MMN deficits
become more pronounced with decreasing probabilityof the deviant stimulus. The effect of deviant proba-bility is assumed to reflect the efficiency by which thestandard stimulus is encoded. Hence the findings in
schizophrenia could be interpreted as indicating defi-
cient encoding—in line with various neuropsycholog-ical studies that reported deficits in encoding inschizophrenia ( Elvevag et al., 2000; Fleming et al.,
1997; Javitt et al., 1999, 1997; Keefe et al., 1997;March et al., 1999; Tek et al., 2002; Wexler et al., 1998 ).
MMN deficits in schizophrenia do not seem to be
related consistently to symptom domains since themajority of the studies investigating this issue did not
find significant correlations. In line with these findingsare the results of the few studies on medication effectsthat could not demonstrate any effect of typical oratypical antipsychotics on MMN despite symptomaticimprovement of patients ( Schall et al., 1998; Umbricht
et al., 1998, 1999 ). Given the strong evidence from a
multitude of studies by cognitive psychologist that
MMN indexes auditory sensory memory and the
operation of a bprimitive intelligence ( Na¨a¨ta¨nen et
al., 2001 ), it is surprising that not much attention has
been given to possible relationships of deficits inMMN to deficits in higher cognitive functions. Theonly laudable exception is a study by Baldeweg et al.
(2004) who found a pronounced association of MMN
deficits and memory impairment. A similar association
of MMN deficits and impairments of episodic memory
has recently been reported by Umbricht et al. (2004) .
The issue if deficient MMN represent a trait marker
or the result of an ongoing disease process resulting inabnormal MMN in chronic patients only remainscontroversial since two studies investigating MMNin first-episode patients failed to find abnormal MMN(Salisbury et al., 2002; Umbricht et al., 2003a ). In
contrast, two of three studies investigating first-degree
relatives found impaired MMN in these subjects(Michie et al., 2002; Bramon et al., 2004; Jessen et
al., 2001 ). Besides differences in the specifics of the
testing paradigms used in these studies, this discrep-ancy may result from a hidden sampling bias: first-episode samples may contain more patients with amore heterogeneous genetic background who show
less neuropathology than chronic patients, go on to
have a favorable outcome and are usually not includedin samples of chronic patients who may be morehomogeneous genetically. Thus, a sampling bias mayskew the results when comparing first-episode tochronic patients and could also explain abnormalitiesin relatives of chronic patients. Only truly prospectivestudies will be able to answer this question. Prelimi-
nary evidence suggests that MMN indeed becomes
abnormal in first-episode patients followed over time(Salisbury et al., 2003 ). Also, this meta-analysis found
some evidence that the extent of deficits in frequencyMMN and possibly duration MMN deficits worsenswith illness progression. Thus, the current evidencesuggests that MMN may be less abnormal or evennormal close to illness onset in some or all patients.D. Umbricht, S. Krljes / Schizophrenia Research 76 (2005) 1–23 18
Thus, on a more technical level, paradigms to
investigate MMN in high-risk, prodromal or first-episode patients will also have to be optimized (seebelow) in order to capture potentially existing, butsubtle abnormalities in MMN generation.
3.1. Conclusions and future directions
Deficits in MMN generation are a robust feature in
chronic schizophrenia. Abnormalities in NMDAreceptor dependent neurotransmission seem to playan important role in MMN deficits. It remainscontroversial, however, if MMN deficits represent atrait marker or develop with illness progression.
Up to date most studies on MMN in schizophrenia
have used paradigms investigating simple deviations
of stimuli with regard to one or two features (i.e.
frequency, duration). Very few studies have employedmore sophisticated stimulation paradigms that wouldallow the investigation of MMN as an index ofstimulus encoding, of processing of more abstractregularities or language related functions and itsrelationship to higher cognitive functions.
Future studies should address:
1. Longitudinal follow-up of prodromal and first-
episode patients, examining the potential progres-sion of MMN deficit in schizophrenia and relatingit to clinical, cognitive, and functional factors.
2. The exact relationship of MMN deficits to
specific cognitive domains, particularly long-termmemory. In paradigms varying the probability of
the deviant stimulus or the difference between
standard and deviant stimulus MMN increases areassumed to reflect the efficiency and accuracy,respectively, of the processes encoding auditoryinformation. Thus, studies using such paradigmscombined with investigations of long-term mem-ory and other cognitive function could generateand test specific hypotheses about the abnormal-
ities underlying specific aspects of higher cogni-
tive functions. Such hypotheses can potentially betested in animal models of human MMN.
3. Given the evidence for an important role of the
NMDA receptor in MMN generation MMN deficitscould become an important endophenotype todefine more homogeneous subsamples of patientsand allow the investigation of potential abnormal-ities in genes coding for the various subunits of the
NDMA receptor in these patient samples.
In addition, it will be important to implement more
sophisticated paradigms that have been tested in detailin healthy subjects. They should include:
1. Paradigms that have been developed to simulta-
neously assess MMN with regard to several feature
dimensions ( Na¨a¨ta¨nen et al., 2004 ).
2. Paradigms investigating short-term plasticity at the
level of the auditory cortex providing insights intomechanisms of learning and memory ( Baldeweg et
al., 2004; Sussman and Winkler, 2001 ).
3. Paradigms assessing not only the processing of
simple features of stimuli, but also of more
complex regularities and language functions
(Kujala et al., 2000; Na ¨a¨ta¨nen et al., 1993, 2001;
Schro˜ger et al., 1996, 1997; Winkler et al., 1999 ).
The goal of using such paradigms should be to
obtain a profile or bsignature Qof MMN deficits in
subjects that will allow to find more subtle abnormal-ities in patients and to obtain a more fine-grained
insight into deficits in information processing at early
cortical stages in schizophrenia. Given the fact that therecording of MMN is not dependent on behavioralperformance, the usual obstacles of matching taskdifficulties between patients and control subjects andcontrolling for potential attentional deficits do nothave to be dealt with. In addition, this advantage alsopermits studies of MMN in animals thus creating an
opportunity to conduct truly translational research.
